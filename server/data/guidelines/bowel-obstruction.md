# Bowel Obstruction — Clinical Consensus

## Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)
- Colicky central abdominal pain
- Vomiting (early, often bilious)
- Abdominal distension
- Absolute constipation (late feature)
- Most common cause: adhesions (from prior surgery)

## Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)
- Colicky lower abdominal pain
- Prominent abdominal distension
- Constipation (early feature)
- Vomiting (late, may be faeculent)
- Most common cause: malignancy

## Other Causes
- Hernias (always examine groins)
- Volvulus (sigmoid or caecal)
- Inflammatory strictures (Crohn's)

## Investigations
- AXR: dilated loops, air-fluid levels
- CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast: gold standard for diagnosis, location, and cause
- Dilated loops: SBO >3cm, LBO >6cm, caecum >9cm (perforation risk)
- Bloods: FBC, U&Es, lactate, CRP, group & save

## Conservative Management ("Drip and Suck")
- Nasogastric tube (NGT) decompression
- IV fluid resuscitation
- Correct electrolyte abnormalities
- Nil by mouth
- Regular reassessment

## Urgent Surgical Indications
- Strangulation
- Closed-loop obstruction
- Perforation
- Peritonitis

## Signs of Strangulation — Do NOT Miss
- Constant (non-colicky) pain
- Localised tenderness with guarding
- Fever
- Tachycardia
- Raised WCC and lactate

## Key Pitfalls
- Missing strangulation by treating conservatively too long
- Not examining groins for hernias
- Diagnosing constipation or gastroenteritis when obstruction is present
- Ignoring caecal diameter >9cm (perforation risk)
