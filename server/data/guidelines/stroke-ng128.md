# NICE NG128 — Stroke and Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)

## Recognition

### Pre-Hospital (FAST Tool)
- Face: has their face fallen on one side?
- Arms: can they raise both arms and keep them there?
- Speech: is their speech slurred?
- Time: time to call 999 if any single sign present

### Emergency Department (ROSIER Score)
- ROSIER (Recognition of Stroke in the Emergency Room) used for clinical assessment
- Assesses: loss of consciousness, seizure activity, asymmetric facial/arm/leg weakness, speech disturbance, visual field defect
- Score >0 suggests stroke is likely

## Acute Imaging
- Immediate non-contrast CT head if patient is a candidate for thrombolysis or thrombectomy
- CT to distinguish ischaemic stroke from haemorrhagic stroke
- CT angiography if large vessel occlusion suspected

## Acute Management — Ischaemic Stroke

### Thrombolysis
- IV alteplase within 4.5 hours of symptom onset
- Earlier treatment = better outcomes
- Exclude haemorrhagic stroke on CT before administration

### Thrombectomy
- Within 6 hours of symptom onset for confirmed large vessel occlusion
- May be considered up to 24 hours if imaging shows salvageable brain tissue
- Can be used alongside or after thrombolysis

### Aspirin
- 300mg aspirin as soon as possible within 24 hours of ischaemic stroke onset
- Continue 300mg daily for 2 weeks, then switch to long-term antithrombotic

### Blood Pressure
- Ischaemic stroke: do NOT actively lower BP unless hypertensive emergency (e.g., hypertensive encephalopathy, aortic dissection, >220/120)
- Haemorrhagic stroke: target systolic BP ≤140 mmHg

### Oxygen
- Only give supplemental oxygen if SpO2 drops below 95%
- Do NOT give routine oxygen therapy

## TIA Management
- Immediate aspirin 300mg
- Specialist assessment and investigation within 24 hours
- Brain imaging (MRI preferred) and carotid imaging
- Risk factor management: antihypertensives, statins, lifestyle

## Common Pitfalls
- Posterior circulation strokes (vertigo, ataxia, visual disturbance) often missed by FAST
- Young patients with stroke may be dismissed
- Haemorrhagic stroke may be worsened by thrombolysis — always CT first
- Delay to thrombolysis significantly reduces benefit
