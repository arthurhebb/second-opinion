# Acute Asthma — BTS/SIGN Guidelines

## Severity Classification
- Moderate: increasing symptoms, PEF 50-75% best or predicted
- Severe: PEF 33-50%, respiratory rate ≥25, heart rate ≥110, cannot complete sentences in one breath
- Life-threatening: PEF <33%, SpO2 <92%, silent chest, cyanosis, bradycardia, hypotension, confusion, exhaustion
- Near-fatal: raised PaCO2 and/or requiring mechanical ventilation

## Immediate Management
- High-flow oxygen: target SpO2 94-98%
- Salbutamol 5mg nebulised (oxygen-driven)
  - Repeat every 15-30 minutes if needed
- Ipratropium bromide 500mcg nebulised: add if severe or life-threatening
- Prednisolone 40-50mg oral (or IV hydrocortisone 100mg if unable to swallow)
  - Continue prednisolone for at least 5 days

## Escalation for Poor Response
- IV magnesium sulfate 1.2-2g over 20 minutes
  - For life-threatening or near-fatal asthma, or poor response to initial treatment
- Aminophylline IV: only if life-threatening and no response to above treatments
  - Requires cardiac monitoring, check theophylline levels

## Assessment
- PEF (peak expiratory flow) — compare to best or predicted
- SpO2
- ABG if SpO2 <92% or life-threatening features
- Normal or rising PaCO2 in acute asthma is alarming (suggests exhaustion)

## Admission Criteria
- Admit ALL patients with severe or life-threatening features
- Observe moderate exacerbations for at least 1 hour after treatment

## Discharge Criteria
- PEF >75% best or predicted
- Stable on inhaled treatment for 12-24 hours
- Inhaler technique checked
- Written asthma action plan
- Follow-up arranged within 48 hours

## Key Pitfalls
- Life-threatening asthma undertreated — exhausted patient may appear calm (silent chest)
- Normal PaCO2 in acute asthma is NOT reassuring — indicates failing ventilation
- Not adding ipratropium in severe/life-threatening attacks
- Premature discharge without adequate observation
- Not giving systemic steroids early
