# NICE NG228 — Subarachnoid Haemorrhage

## When to Suspect
- Maintain a high index of suspicion for sudden severe headache
- **Thunderclap headache**: reaches maximum intensity within 1-5 minutes
- Often described as "worst headache of my life"
- May be associated with: neck stiffness, photophobia, nausea/vomiting, altered consciousness, seizures
- Can present with collapse or sudden death

## Urgent Investigation

### CT Head (Non-Contrast)
- Urgent non-contrast CT head is first-line investigation
- CT is most accurate when performed within 6 hours of symptom onset (sensitivity approaches 100%)
- Sensitivity decreases with time — after 6 hours, a negative CT does not exclude SAH

### If CT is Negative

#### Within 6 hours of onset
- If CT is negative and performed within 6 hours by experienced neuroradiologist: do NOT routinely perform lumbar puncture
- Seek specialist neurosurgical/neurovascular advice for further management

#### After 6 hours from onset
- If CT is negative and >6 hours since onset: consider lumbar puncture
- LP should be performed ≥12 hours after headache onset (to allow xanthochromia to develop)
- Look for xanthochromia (bilirubin) in CSF by spectrophotometry

### CT Angiography
- CT angiography (CTA) to identify the source of bleeding (aneurysm)
- Perform urgently once SAH is confirmed

## Key Management Principles
- Rebleeding risk is highest within the first 24 hours — urgent neurosurgical referral
- Definitive treatment: coiling (endovascular) or clipping (surgical) of the aneurysm
- Nimodipine 60mg every 4 hours for 21 days (prevents vasospasm/delayed cerebral ischaemia)
- Maintain normovolaemia
- Monitor for complications: rebleeding, vasospasm, hydrocephalus, seizures, hyponatraemia

## Red Flags for Immediate Action
- Reduced GCS or deteriorating consciousness
- New focal neurological deficit (may indicate vasospasm or rebleeding)
- Seizures
- Signs of raised intracranial pressure

## Common Pitfalls
- Dismissing thunderclap headache as migraine or tension headache
- Relying on negative CT performed >6 hours after onset without further investigation
- Not performing LP at least 12 hours after onset (xanthochromia may not yet be present)
- Missing "sentinel headache" (minor warning leak days before major SAH)
- Attributing symptoms to meningitis without considering SAH
