# NICE CG134 — Anaphylaxis

## Definition
- Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening, generalised or systemic hypersensitivity reaction
- Characterised by rapidly developing airway, breathing, or circulation problems
- Usually associated with skin and mucosal changes (urticaria, angioedema) — but skin changes may be absent

## Recognition
- Sudden onset and rapid progression of symptoms
- Airway: swelling of tongue, throat, stridor, hoarse voice
- Breathing: wheeze, dyspnoea, cyanosis, respiratory arrest
- Circulation: hypotension, tachycardia, pallor, cardiac arrest
- Skin/mucosal: urticaria, erythema, angioedema (may be absent in up to 20%)
- Gastrointestinal: abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhoea

## Acute Treatment

### First-Line: IM Adrenaline
- IM adrenaline (1:1000) into anterolateral thigh — do NOT delay
- Adult dose: 500 micrograms (0.5ml of 1:1000)
- Repeat after 5 minutes if no improvement
- IV adrenaline only by experienced specialists in monitored settings

### Supportive Measures
- High-flow oxygen
- IV fluid bolus for hypotension (crystalloid 500-1000ml rapidly)
- Position: lying flat with legs raised if hypotensive (sitting up if breathing difficulty)
- Nebulised salbutamol for bronchospasm
- IV chlorphenamine and IV hydrocortisone are second-line adjuncts (not substitutes for adrenaline)

## Investigation
- Timed mast cell tryptase samples:
  - As soon as possible after emergency treatment started
  - 1-2 hours after symptom onset
  - Baseline sample at ≥24 hours or in follow-up clinic
- Elevated tryptase supports diagnosis of anaphylaxis

## Observation Period
- Adults: observe for 6-12 hours after symptom resolution
- Biphasic reactions can occur within 72 hours of initial reaction
- Longer observation if severe reaction, slow onset, history of biphasic reactions, or difficult to access emergency care

## Discharge and Follow-Up
- Prescribe two adrenaline auto-injectors on discharge
- Train patient in auto-injector use before discharge
- Written anaphylaxis emergency action plan
- Referral to allergy clinic for investigation of trigger
- Advise about biphasic reaction risk

## Common Pitfalls
- Delayed or withheld adrenaline (most common cause of fatal anaphylaxis)
- Using IV adrenaline without proper monitoring
- Relying on antihistamines/steroids instead of adrenaline
- Discharging without auto-injectors or allergy referral
- Missing anaphylaxis when skin changes are absent
