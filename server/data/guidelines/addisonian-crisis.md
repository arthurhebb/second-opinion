# Addisonian Crisis (Acute Adrenal Insufficiency)

## Overview
- Life-threatening emergency caused by acute cortisol deficiency
- Can occur in known adrenal insufficiency (primary or secondary) or as first presentation of undiagnosed Addison's disease
- Often precipitated by physiological stress: infection, surgery, trauma, vomiting/diarrhoea, or missed steroid doses

## Recognition
- **Hypotension** (often profound, refractory to fluids and vasopressors without steroids)
- Hypovolaemia and dehydration
- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (may mimic acute abdomen)
- Confusion, reduced consciousness, lethargy
- Fever (may be due to crisis itself or precipitating infection)
- Weakness, fatigue

## Classic Biochemistry
- **Hyponatraemia** (sodium low — impaired free water excretion)
- **Hyperkalaemia** (potassium high — aldosterone deficiency in primary adrenal insufficiency)
- **Hypoglycaemia** (impaired gluconeogenesis)
- Elevated urea/creatinine (pre-renal AKI from hypovolaemia)
- Note: in secondary adrenal insufficiency, potassium may be normal (aldosterone preserved)

## Clues to Chronic Addison's Disease
- Hyperpigmentation (skin creases, buccal mucosa, scars) — due to elevated ACTH
- Weight loss, chronic fatigue
- History of autoimmune conditions (thyroid disease, type 1 diabetes, vitiligo)
- Previous prolonged steroid use (secondary adrenal insufficiency — no hyperpigmentation)

## Emergency Management

### Immediate Treatment — Do NOT Wait for Results
- **IV hydrocortisone 100mg bolus** immediately
- Then hydrocortisone 50-100mg every 6-8 hours (or continuous infusion)
- **IV 0.9% saline** — aggressive fluid resuscitation (1L rapidly, then reassess)
- **IV glucose** (10% or 20% dextrose) if hypoglycaemic
- Treat precipitating cause (e.g., antibiotics for infection)

### Blood Tests (Take Before Treatment if Possible)
- Random serum cortisol (a low random cortisol during physiological stress is diagnostic)
- Plasma ACTH (distinguishes primary from secondary)
- U&Es, glucose, FBC, CRP, blood cultures
- Do NOT delay treatment to obtain these samples

## Diagnosis (Once Stable)
- Short Synacthen test (SST): definitive test for adrenal insufficiency
- Perform when patient is stable and off hydrocortisone (can switch to dexamethasone temporarily as it does not interfere with cortisol assay)
- Adrenal autoantibodies (21-hydroxylase) for primary Addison's

## Common Pitfalls
- Not considering adrenal crisis in unexplained hypotension with hyponatraemia
- Waiting for cortisol results before giving hydrocortisone — this is a clinical emergency
- Attributing symptoms to sepsis alone without considering coexisting adrenal insufficiency
- Missing adrenal crisis in patients on long-term steroids who have stopped or not increased dose during illness
- Not recognising that abdominal pain and vomiting can be features of the crisis itself
- Hyperkalaemia treated without considering underlying cause
