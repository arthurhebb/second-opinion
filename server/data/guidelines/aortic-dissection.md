# Aortic Dissection

## Overview
- Tear in the intima of the aorta allowing blood to track into the media
- High mortality if not rapidly diagnosed and treated
- Stanford classification: Type A (involves ascending aorta), Type B (descending aorta only)

## Recognition
- **Sudden onset tearing or ripping chest pain**, often radiating to the back (interscapular)
- Pain is maximal at onset (unlike MI which builds gradually)
- Blood pressure differential between arms (>20 mmHg systolic) — important clinical sign
- New aortic regurgitation murmur (diastolic, best heard at right sternal edge)
- Hypertension is common; hypotension suggests rupture or cardiac tamponade

## Atypical Presentations
- May present as acute stroke (if dissection occludes carotid/vertebral arteries)
- Acute limb ischaemia (if dissection occludes peripheral arteries)
- MI mimic (if dissection involves coronary ostia — particularly right coronary)
- Abdominal pain (if dissection extends to mesenteric vessels)
- Syncope, heart failure, or cardiac arrest

## Investigations
- **CT aortogram (CTA)** is the diagnostic investigation of choice — fast and widely available
- D-dimer is often elevated — may cause confusion with PE (but a negative D-dimer has high negative predictive value)
- ECG: may show LVH, ischaemic changes, or be normal
- Chest X-ray: widened mediastinum (present in only ~60%), but may be normal
- Transoesophageal echocardiography (TOE) is an alternative in unstable patients

## Management

### Type A (Ascending Aorta)
- **Emergency cardiothoracic surgery** — mortality increases ~1-2% per hour without intervention
- Transfer to cardiothoracic centre immediately
- Control heart rate and blood pressure while awaiting surgery (IV labetalol or esmolol, target HR <60, systolic <120)

### Type B (Descending Aorta)
- Medical management is first-line: aggressive blood pressure and heart rate control
- IV beta-blockers (labetalol, esmolol)
- Target systolic BP 100-120 mmHg
- Endovascular repair (TEVAR) for complicated Type B (malperfusion, rupture, refractory pain)

## Common Pitfalls
- Misdiagnosed as acute coronary syndrome — thrombolysis/anticoagulation in dissection is catastrophic
- Misdiagnosed as PE due to elevated D-dimer
- Normal chest X-ray does not exclude dissection
- Failure to check bilateral arm blood pressures
- Not considering dissection in young patients with Marfan syndrome or connective tissue disorders
- Pain attributed to musculoskeletal cause
