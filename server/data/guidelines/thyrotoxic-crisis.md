# Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxic Crisis) — Clinical Consensus

## Definition
- Life-threatening exacerbation of thyrotoxicosis
- Medical emergency with high mortality if untreated

## Triggers
- Infection (most common)
- Surgery
- Trauma
- Radioiodine therapy
- Iodinated contrast media

## Clinical Features
- High fever (>38.5 degrees C)
- Tachycardia out of proportion to fever
- Agitation, delirium, psychosis
- GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal pain)
- Cardiac failure (high-output)
- Seizures (late)

## Diagnosis
- Clinical diagnosis — do not wait for thyroid function results
- Burch-Wartofsky Point Scale (score >45 highly suggestive)

## Emergency Management
1. High-dose antithyroid drug: propylthiouracil (PTU) or carbimazole
2. Beta-blocker: propranolol (also blocks peripheral T4 to T3 conversion)
3. IV hydrocortisone 100mg QDS:
   - Blocks T4 to T3 conversion
   - Treats possible concurrent adrenal insufficiency
4. Cooling measures for hyperthermia:
   - Avoid aspirin — displaces T4 from binding proteins, worsening thyrotoxicosis
   - Use paracetamol and physical cooling
5. Lugol's iodine: give 1 hour AFTER antithyroid drug
   - Blocks further thyroid hormone release
   - Must not be given before antithyroid drug

## Supportive Care
- ITU/HDU admission
- IV fluids
- Treat precipitant (e.g. antibiotics for infection)
- Cardiac monitoring

## Key Pitfalls
- Missing thyroid storm — attributing features to sepsis or psychiatric presentation
- Giving aspirin for fever (worsens thyrotoxicosis)
- Giving Lugol's iodine before antithyroid drug (fuels further hormone synthesis)
- Not giving hydrocortisone
