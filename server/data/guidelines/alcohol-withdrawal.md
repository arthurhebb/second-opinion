# Alcohol Withdrawal / Delirium Tremens — NICE CG100

## Timeline After Last Drink
- 6-24 hours: tremor, anxiety, sweating, nausea, tachycardia, hypertension
- 12-48 hours: seizures (typically generalised tonic-clonic)
- 48-72 hours: delirium tremens

## Delirium Tremens
- Confusion, disorientation
- Visual and auditory hallucinations
- Autonomic instability (fever, tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis)
- Mortality 5-15% if untreated
- Medical emergency

## Severity Assessment
- CIWA-Ar (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol — revised) scale
- Regular monitoring and scoring

## Pharmacological Management
- Benzodiazepines are the mainstay of treatment
  - Chlordiazepoxide: planned/elective withdrawal (reducing regimen)
  - Diazepam or lorazepam: acute/severe withdrawal or delirium tremens
- Symptom-triggered dosing often preferred over fixed-dose

## Thiamine — Critical
- IV Pabrinex (B vitamins including thiamine) to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy
- Give BEFORE any glucose administration
- High-dose IV Pabrinex (2-3 pairs TDS for 3-5 days) if malnourished or suspected Wernicke's

## Supportive Care
- Assess and correct: dehydration, electrolytes (Mg, PO4, K), hypoglycaemia
- Quiet, well-lit environment
- Regular observations
- Avoid additional sedating medications where possible

## Admission Criteria
- CIWA-Ar score >15
- Seizures
- Delirium tremens
- Significant medical comorbidity
- No safe home environment for managed withdrawal
- Previous complicated withdrawal

## Key Pitfalls
- Not eliciting alcohol history — delirium tremens mistaken for sepsis
- Giving glucose before thiamine (precipitates Wernicke's)
- Underestimating severity — delirium tremens can be fatal
- Not replacing magnesium and phosphate
- Inadequate benzodiazepine dosing in severe withdrawal
