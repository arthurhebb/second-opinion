# Testicular Torsion

## Overview
- Twisting of the spermatic cord causing ischaemia of the testis
- Urological emergency — time-critical, with a 6-hour window for optimal salvage
- Peak incidence: 12-18 years, but can occur at any age (including neonates)

## Recognition
- Acute onset severe scrotal pain — often wakes patient from sleep
- Nausea and vomiting (common, due to visceral pain)
- Lower abdominal pain may be the presenting complaint (referred pain)
- Pain does NOT improve with elevation of the testis (negative Prehn's sign)

## Key Clinical Signs
- High-riding testicle (retracted upward in the scrotum)
- Transverse lie of the testis
- Absent cremasteric reflex — most sensitive clinical sign
- Swollen, tender, erythematous scrotum
- Bell-clapper deformity (testis hangs freely) predisposes to torsion

## Time-Critical Management
- **6-hour window**: testicular salvage rate ~90-100% if detorsion within 6 hours
- 6-12 hours: salvage rate drops to ~50%
- Beyond 24 hours: salvage rate <10%, orchidectomy likely required
- **Do NOT delay for imaging if clinical suspicion is high** — proceed directly to surgical exploration

## Investigation
- Clinical diagnosis is sufficient to proceed to surgery
- Doppler ultrasound ONLY if diagnosis uncertain and will not cause significant delay
- Ultrasound may show reduced or absent blood flow to the affected testis
- A normal ultrasound does not exclude torsion

## Surgical Management
- Emergency scrotal exploration and detorsion
- If testis is viable: orchidopexy (fixation) of both testes
- If testis is non-viable: orchidectomy + fixation of the contralateral testis
- Bilateral fixation is essential to prevent contralateral torsion

## Common Pitfalls
- Misdiagnosed as epididymitis — especially in adolescents (epididymitis is rare in this age group)
- Referred abdominal pain leads to investigation for appendicitis or other abdominal pathology
- Waiting for ultrasound when clinical picture is suggestive — delays definitive treatment
- Assuming pain relief after spontaneous detorsion means the problem is resolved (recurrence risk is high)
- Not examining the scrotum in any male patient with acute lower abdominal pain
- Intermittent torsion presenting with recurrent self-resolving episodes — still needs referral
