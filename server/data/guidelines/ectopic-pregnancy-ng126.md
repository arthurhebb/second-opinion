# NICE NG126 — Ectopic Pregnancy and Miscarriage

## Key Principles
- Ectopic pregnancy is a potentially life-threatening condition
- Atypical presentation is common — do not rely on classic triad alone
- Always consider ectopic pregnancy in any woman of reproductive age with abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, or collapse

## Recognition
- Classic presentation: abdominal/pelvic pain, vaginal bleeding, amenorrhoea — but often incomplete
- Shoulder tip pain is a classic sign of intra-abdominal bleeding (diaphragmatic irritation)
- Gastrointestinal symptoms (diarrhoea, painful defecation) may mimic other conditions
- Dizziness, fainting, or collapse may indicate rupture

## Investigations

### Pregnancy Test
- Perform a pregnancy test in ALL women of reproductive age presenting with abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, or collapse
- Urine hCG is rapid and sensitive

### Transvaginal Ultrasound
- First-line imaging for suspected ectopic pregnancy
- Look for adnexal mass, free fluid, absence of intrauterine pregnancy

### Serial hCG Monitoring
- Rise of >63% over 48 hours suggests viable intrauterine pregnancy
- Fall of >50% over 48 hours suggests pregnancy loss (failing pregnancy)
- Suboptimal rise or plateau: suspect ectopic pregnancy
- Single hCG value cannot reliably locate pregnancy

## Red Flags — Immediate Action
- Haemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension, collapse) → transfer to A&E immediately
- Signs of significant intra-abdominal bleeding
- Severe or worsening pain with positive pregnancy test

## Management

### Expectant Management
- Selected cases of confirmed ectopic with declining hCG and no symptoms
- Close monitoring with serial hCG required

### Medical Management (Methotrexate)
- Single-dose IM methotrexate for suitable patients
- Criteria: haemodynamically stable, no significant pain, unruptured ectopic, hCG typically <5000
- Follow-up hCG monitoring essential

### Surgical Management
- Laparoscopic salpingectomy is preferred surgical approach
- Salpingotomy if contralateral tube damaged or absent
- Emergency surgery for ruptured ectopic or haemodynamic instability

## Common Pitfalls
- Ectopic pregnancy misdiagnosed as gastroenteritis, UTI, or appendicitis
- Failure to perform pregnancy test in women of reproductive age
- Assuming negative ultrasound excludes ectopic (early ectopic may not be visible)
- Relying on single hCG value rather than trend
