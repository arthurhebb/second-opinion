# Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

## Diagnostic Criteria — All Three Required
- **Hyperglycaemia**: blood glucose >11 mmol/L (or known diabetes)
- **Ketonaemia**: blood ketones >3 mmol/L (or significant ketonuria ≥2+)
- **Acidosis**: venous pH <7.3 or bicarbonate <15 mmol/L

## Severity Assessment
- Mild: pH 7.2-7.3, bicarbonate 10-15
- Moderate: pH 7.1-7.2, bicarbonate 5-10
- Severe: pH <7.1, bicarbonate <5, or reduced consciousness, SpO2 <92%, systolic BP <90, HR >100 or <60

## Immediate Management

### Fluid Replacement
- IV 0.9% sodium chloride (isotonic saline)
- Rapid initial bolus (500ml over 15 minutes) ONLY if systolic BP <90 mmHg
- Otherwise: 1L over first hour, then 1L over next 2 hours, then 1L over next 2 hours, then 1L over next 4 hours
- Adjust based on clinical assessment and fluid status
- Do NOT give fluids too rapidly except in circulatory collapse

### Insulin
- Fixed-rate IV insulin infusion (FRII) at 0.1 units/kg/hour
- Do NOT give bolus IV insulin
- Continue long-acting basal insulin if patient normally takes it
- Aim for glucose fall of ~3 mmol/L/hour and ketone fall of ≥0.5 mmol/L/hour

### Potassium Replacement
- Start potassium replacement early — insulin drives potassium intracellularly
- K+ >5.5: nil potassium in fluids
- K+ 3.5-5.5: 40 mmol/L KCl in each litre of fluid
- K+ <3.5: senior review, higher replacement needed, delay insulin if severe hypokalaemia
- Monitor potassium at least every 1-2 hours initially

### Glucose Management
- When blood glucose falls to 10-15 mmol/L: add 10% glucose infusion alongside saline
- Continue insulin infusion — do NOT stop insulin just because glucose has normalised (ketosis may persist)

## Do NOT
- Give IV bicarbonate routinely (may worsen intracellular acidosis and hypokalaemia)
- Stop insulin infusion based on glucose alone — use ketone clearance as guide
- Forget VTE prophylaxis (DKA is a prothrombotic state)

## Monitoring
- Hourly blood glucose and ketones
- Venous blood gas at 1, 2, 4, 6 hours minimum
- Potassium every 1-2 hours
- Fluid balance chart
- Cardiac monitoring if potassium abnormal

## Resolution Criteria
- pH >7.3, bicarbonate >15 mmol/L, ketones <0.6 mmol/L
- Patient eating and drinking
- Transition to subcutaneous insulin (overlap IV and SC by 30-60 minutes)

## Common Pitfalls
- Missing DKA in euglycaemic presentations (especially SGLT2 inhibitor users)
- Inadequate potassium monitoring leading to dangerous hypokalaemia
- Stopping insulin when glucose normalises but ketones persist
- Fluid overload in elderly or those with cardiac/renal disease
- Not identifying the precipitant (infection, missed insulin, new diagnosis)
