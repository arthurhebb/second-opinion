# Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS) — Clinical Consensus

## Characteristics
- Typically type 2 diabetes, often elderly patients
- Severe hyperglycaemia: glucose usually >30 mmol/L
- High serum osmolality: >320 mosmol/kg
- NO significant ketosis (distinguishes from DKA)
- Develops over days to weeks (unlike DKA which is acute)

## Key Difference from DKA
- HHS: gradual onset, profound dehydration, high osmolality, no significant ketones
- DKA: acute onset, ketoacidosis, often younger/type 1

## Fluid Deficit
- Profound dehydration, often 100-220 ml/kg deficit
- May be 6-12 litres fluid deficit

## Management
- IV 0.9% sodium chloride: aggressive but careful fluid replacement
- Insulin at LOW rate: 0.05 units/kg/hr
  - Do NOT use high-rate DKA insulin protocol (0.1 units/kg/hr)
  - Fluid replacement alone often reduces glucose significantly
- Only start insulin once glucose is no longer falling with fluids alone, or immediately if significant ketonaemia

## Treatment Targets
- Aim to reduce glucose by approximately 5 mmol/L per hour
- Aim to reduce osmolality by 3-8 mosmol/kg per hour
- Avoid rapid changes — risk of cerebral oedema

## VTE Prophylaxis
- Essential — high thrombotic risk in HHS
- LMWH unless contraindicated

## Monitoring
- Hourly glucose, U&Es, osmolality calculations
- Fluid balance (urinary catheter often needed)
- Watch for cerebral oedema if osmolality drops too fast
- Cardiac monitoring

## Prognosis
- Mortality approximately 20% (much higher than DKA at 1-5%)
- Often reflects frail, comorbid patient population

## Key Pitfalls
- Treating with DKA-rate insulin (too aggressive, causes dangerous osmolality shifts)
- Inadequate fluid replacement
- Not giving VTE prophylaxis
- Rapid correction causing cerebral oedema
- Not identifying and treating the precipitant
