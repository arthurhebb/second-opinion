# Necrotising Fasciitis

## Overview
- Rapidly progressive, life-threatening infection of the deep fascia and subcutaneous tissue
- Mortality 20-40% even with treatment; significantly higher if delayed
- Types: Type I (polymicrobial), Type II (monomicrobial — Group A Streptococcus most common)
- Can affect any body site; limbs, perineum (Fournier's gangrene), and abdominal wall most common

## Recognition — Key Red Flags
- **Pain out of proportion to clinical findings** — the most important early sign
- Rapidly progressive erythema, swelling, and induration
- Systemic toxicity: fever, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion (sepsis features)
- Skin changes progressing from erythema to dusky/purple discolouration to necrosis
- Bullae or blistering
- Crepitus on palpation (subcutaneous gas — late sign, not always present)
- **Skin may appear relatively NORMAL in early stages** — diagnosis is clinical

## Risk Factors
- Diabetes mellitus
- Immunosuppression (steroids, chemotherapy, HIV)
- Obesity
- Recent surgery or trauma (including minor skin breaks)
- IV drug use
- Peripheral vascular disease
- Chronic liver or kidney disease

## Investigation

### LRINEC Score (Laboratory Risk Indicator for Necrotising Fasciitis)
- CRP, WCC, haemoglobin, sodium, creatinine, glucose
- Score ≥6: suspicious — score ≥8: strongly predictive
- Useful to support clinical suspicion but should NOT delay surgical referral

### Imaging
- CT or MRI may show gas tracking along fascial planes, fascial thickening, or fluid collections
- Imaging should NOT delay surgical exploration if clinical suspicion is high
- Plain X-ray may show subcutaneous gas

### Blood Tests
- FBC, U&Es, CRP, lactate, blood cultures, clotting, blood gas
- Often shows markedly elevated WCC and CRP, AKI, metabolic acidosis

## Management

### Surgical — Definitive Treatment
- **Emergency surgical debridement** — the single most important intervention
- Often requires multiple returns to theatre for further debridement
- Do not wait for imaging or lab confirmation if clinical suspicion is high
- Delay in surgery is the strongest predictor of mortality

### Medical
- **Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics**: typically piperacillin-tazobactam or meropenem
- **Clindamycin** added specifically for toxin suppression (inhibits bacterial protein/toxin production)
- IV fluid resuscitation
- Critical care support (vasopressors, organ support as needed)
- VTE prophylaxis

## Common Pitfalls
- Misdiagnosed as cellulitis — cellulitis should not cause pain out of proportion or systemic toxicity
- Waiting for imaging or lab results before surgical referral
- Assuming normal-looking skin excludes the diagnosis (deep infection may not be visible)
- Not recognising perineal necrotising fasciitis (Fournier's gangrene)
- Underestimating rate of progression — reassess frequently
