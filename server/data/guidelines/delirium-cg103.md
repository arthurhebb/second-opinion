# Delirium — NICE CG103

## Risk Factors
- Age ≥65 years
- Pre-existing cognitive impairment or dementia
- Hip fracture
- Severe illness (ICU admission)

## Daily Assessment — Watch For
- Worsened concentration
- Slow responses
- New or worsening confusion
- Hallucinations
- Restlessness or agitation
- Sleep disturbance
- Withdrawal or reduced mobility

## Hypoactive Delirium (Often Missed)
- Withdrawal from social interaction
- Slow responses
- Reduced mobility and activity
- Excessive sleepiness
- Often misattributed to dementia, depression, or tiredness

## Screening Tools
- 4AT tool (general hospital settings)
- CAM-ICU (critical care)

## Investigate Underlying Causes
- Dehydration
- Constipation
- Hypoxia
- Infection
- Immobility
- Uncontrolled pain
- Medications (especially anticholinergics, opioids, benzodiazepines)
- Poor nutrition
- Sensory impairment (missing glasses/hearing aids)

## Prevention
- Orientation aids (clocks, calendars, familiar objects)
- Cognitive stimulation
- Early mobilisation
- Adequate hydration and nutrition
- Sleep hygiene (reduce noise, avoid unnecessary overnight observations)

## Management
- Treat the underlying cause first and foremost
- Non-pharmacological de-escalation
- Short-term haloperidol ONLY if de-escalation fails (≤1 week, lowest effective dose)
- Avoid benzodiazepines unless alcohol withdrawal or Lewy body dementia

## Key Pitfalls
- Missing hypoactive delirium (quiet, withdrawn patients)
- Attributing delirium to dementia without investigating reversible causes
- Not checking medications as a cause
- Over-sedation with pharmacological agents
