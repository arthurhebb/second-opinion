# Acute Pancreatitis — NICE NG104

## Presentation
- Sudden-onset abdominal pain (often epigastric, radiating to back)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Epigastric tenderness
- May have guarding

## Diagnosis
- Blood lipase or amylase (usually raised >3x upper limit of normal)
- CT abdomen if enzymes not elevated but clinical suspicion high
- Do NOT assume alcohol-related — investigate other causes (gallstones, drugs, metabolic)

## Severity Assessment
- Moderately severe: organ failure resolving within 48 hours, or local complications
- Severe: organ failure persisting >48 hours
- Assess using validated scoring systems (e.g. Glasgow, APACHE II)

## Nutrition
- Avoid nil-by-mouth — oral intake as tolerated
- Enteral nutrition within 72 hours for moderate-severe pancreatitis
- Nasojejunal feeding if oral intake not possible

## Antimicrobials
- Do NOT use prophylactic antimicrobials
- Antibiotics only for confirmed infected necrosis or concurrent infection

## Specialist Referral
- Refer to specialist centre if necrotic, infective, or haemorrhagic complications
- Multidisciplinary approach for severe pancreatitis

## Aetiology Investigation
- Ultrasound for gallstones
- Alcohol history
- Drug history
- Lipid profile
- Calcium levels

## Key Pitfalls
- Assuming alcohol-related without investigating gallstones and other causes
- Keeping patient nil-by-mouth (outdated practice)
- Using prophylactic antibiotics (no benefit, promotes resistance)
- Underestimating severity — persistent organ failure defines severe disease
