# Cauda Equina Syndrome

## Overview
- Compression of the cauda equina nerve roots (below the conus medullaris, typically L2 and below)
- Surgical emergency — delay in treatment leads to permanent neurological deficit
- Most commonly caused by large central disc herniation, but also tumours, abscess, haematoma

## Red Flags — When to Suspect
- Bilateral leg pain, weakness, or numbness (may be asymmetric)
- Saddle anaesthesia: numbness/altered sensation in perineum, buttocks, inner thighs
- Bladder dysfunction: urinary retention, loss of sensation of bladder fullness, incontinence
- Bowel dysfunction: faecal incontinence, loss of anal tone
- Progressive neurological deficit in the lower limbs
- Sexual dysfunction (loss of sensation)

## Key Clinical Signs
- Reduced or absent perianal/saddle sensation
- Reduced anal tone on PR examination — this is a critical finding
- Urinary retention with overflow incontinence (post-void residual >200ml is significant)
- Bilateral lower limb neurological signs (weakness, reflex changes, sensory loss)
- Urinary retention is a LATE sign — do not wait for it to develop before investigating

## Investigation
- **Emergency MRI of the whole spine** — this is the investigation of choice
- Do NOT delay MRI for other investigations
- If MRI unavailable: urgent transfer to a centre with MRI capability
- CT myelogram is an alternative if MRI is contraindicated

## Management
- Emergency surgical decompression — ideally within 48 hours of symptom onset (sooner is better)
- Outcomes are significantly worse with delay beyond 48 hours
- Complete cauda equina syndrome (established urinary retention) has worse prognosis than incomplete
- Post-operative: bladder catheterisation, physiotherapy, neuropathic pain management

## Common Pitfalls
- Back pain attributed to mechanical/musculoskeletal cause without screening for red flags
- Not asking about bladder and bowel function in patients with back pain
- Not performing PR examination when cauda equina is suspected
- Waiting for urinary retention to develop before referring (this is a late sign)
- Incomplete neurological examination of lower limbs
- Delay in MRI due to out-of-hours presentation or lack of clinical urgency
- Missing progressive symptoms over days (insidious onset variant)
