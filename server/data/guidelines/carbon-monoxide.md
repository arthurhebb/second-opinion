# Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

## Overview
- Carbon monoxide (CO) binds haemoglobin with ~250x greater affinity than oxygen
- Causes tissue hypoxia despite apparently normal oxygen saturations
- Common source: faulty gas boilers, heaters, fires, vehicle exhaust, barbecues in enclosed spaces
- More common in winter months

## Recognition — Non-Specific Symptoms
- Headache (most common early symptom)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Dizziness and weakness
- Confusion, altered mental state
- Chest pain (may mimic ACS)
- Loss of consciousness, seizures (severe cases)
- Cherry-red skin discolouration is a rare and late sign — do not rely on this

## Key Diagnostic Clues
- **Multiple household members affected simultaneously** — very strong clue
- Symptoms improve when away from the exposure source
- Winter presentation with gas appliances in the home
- Symptoms in a house with a recently serviced or faulty boiler

## Investigation

### Pulse Oximetry Limitation
- Standard pulse oximetry reads FALSELY NORMAL — it cannot distinguish carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb) from oxyhaemoglobin
- Do NOT rely on SpO2 to exclude CO poisoning

### Definitive Diagnosis
- Arterial or venous blood gas with **co-oximetry** — measures COHb level directly
- COHb >3% in non-smokers or >10% in smokers is significant
- COHb >10%: symptomatic poisoning
- COHb >25%: severe poisoning, risk of cardiac and neurological complications
- COHb >40%: life-threatening
- Note: COHb levels may have fallen by the time of testing if patient has been removed from exposure

### Additional Investigations
- ECG (CO can cause myocardial ischaemia and arrhythmias)
- Troponin if chest pain or ECG changes
- Blood lactate (tissue hypoxia)
- Pregnancy test in women of reproductive age (fetus is highly vulnerable)

## Management
- Remove from exposure source immediately
- **High-flow 100% oxygen via non-rebreather mask** at 15L/min
- Continue until COHb <5% and symptoms resolved (typically 4-6 hours)
- Half-life of COHb: ~320 minutes on room air, ~80 minutes on 100% O2, ~23 minutes with hyperbaric O2

### Consider Hyperbaric Oxygen
- Loss of consciousness
- Neurological signs
- Cardiac complications
- Pregnancy
- COHb >25%

## Common Pitfalls
- Relying on normal pulse oximetry to exclude CO poisoning
- Not considering CO poisoning in patients with vague symptoms (headache, nausea)
- Missing the diagnosis when only one household member presents
- Not testing for CO in confused or collapsed patients found at home in winter
- Discharging without ensuring the CO source has been identified and addressed
- Delayed neurological syndrome: neurological deterioration days to weeks after apparent recovery
