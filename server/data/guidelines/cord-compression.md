# Metastatic Spinal Cord Compression — NICE CG75

## Red Flags
- Severe or progressively worsening back pain
- Band-like pain around the trunk
- Limb weakness (upper or lower)
- Sensory changes (numbness, paraesthesia)
- Bladder or bowel dysfunction (retention, incontinence)

## Key Warning
- Pain often precedes neurological deficit by weeks
- Suspect in ANY cancer patient with new back pain
- Early diagnosis dramatically improves functional outcome

## Emergency Imaging
- MRI whole spine within 24 hours
- Ideally within 4-6 hours if neurological deficit present
- MRI whole spine (not just symptomatic level — multiple levels may be affected)

## Immediate Management
- High-dose dexamethasone 16mg IMMEDIATELY while awaiting imaging
- Do not delay steroids for imaging
- Analgesia (often severe pain)
- Flat bed rest until spinal stability confirmed

## Definitive Treatment
- Surgery: decompression with or without spinal stabilisation
- Radiotherapy: if surgery not appropriate
- Decision depends on: tumour type, level, extent, patient fitness, prognosis

## Functional Outcome
- Neurological status at time of treatment is the strongest predictor of outcome
- Patients ambulant at treatment have best chance of remaining ambulant
- Once paralysis established for >24-48 hours, recovery is unlikely

## Key Pitfalls
- Attributing back pain to metastatic bone pain alone (without considering cord compression)
- Delayed MRI — every hour matters once deficit develops
- Not giving dexamethasone immediately
- Imaging only the symptomatic level (missing additional levels)
- Not considering cord compression in patients with known cancer
