# NICE NG240 — Meningitis and Meningococcal Disease

## Recognition of Bacterial Meningitis

### Red Flag Combination
- Fever, headache, neck stiffness, and altered level of consciousness = strong suspicion
- Note: fever and neck stiffness less common in babies

### Additional Symptoms Suggesting Meningitis
- Bulging fontanelle (babies/young children)
- Altered consciousness or cognition
- Focal neurological deficits
- Photophobia
- Seizures
- Non-blanching petechial/purpuric rash
- Unexplained body pain (limb, back, abdominal)

### High-Risk Groups
- Missed meningococcal, Hib, or pneumococcal vaccinations
- Reduced/absent spleen function
- Complement deficiency
- Students in large shared accommodation
- Previous meningitis/meningococcal disease
- CSF leak
- Cochlear implant

## Recognition of Meningococcal Disease

### Red Flags (Strongly Suspect)
- Haemorrhagic, non-blanching rash with lesions >2mm
- Rapidly progressive/spreading non-blanching petechial or purpuric rash
- Meningitis symptoms combined with non-blanching rash

### Critical Guidance
- Do NOT rule out disease based on absence of rash
- Rashes difficult to detect on darker skin — check conjunctivae
- Rash may progress from blanching to non-blanching

### Non-Specific Severe Illness Indicators
- Pale, mottled skin or cyanosis
- Lethargy/does not stay awake when roused
- Cold hands and feet
- Low blood pressure (age-specific)
- High heart rate (age-specific)
- Capillary refill time ≥3 seconds
- Reduced urine output

## Pre-Hospital Management
- Emergency transfer for all suspected cases
- Notify hospital: patient needs senior clinical decision maker
- Do NOT delay transfer to administer antibiotics
- For strongly suspected bacterial meningitis with significant delay: give IV/IM ceftriaxone or benzylpenicillin
- For strongly suspected meningococcal disease: give ceftriaxone/benzylpenicillin ASAP unless delays transfer

## Hospital Investigations — Bacterial Meningitis
- Antibiotics within 1 hour of hospital arrival
- Blood tests and lumbar puncture before antibiotics if safe and won't delay therapy

### Blood Tests Required
- Blood culture
- WBC (including neutrophils)
- CRP or procalcitonin
- Blood glucose
- Whole-blood diagnostic PCR (meningococcal, pneumococcal)

### Important
- Do NOT rule out bacterial meningitis based only on normal CRP, PCT, or white cell count

### Neuroimaging Before LP
- Do NOT routinely perform before lumbar puncture
- Perform if: new focal neurological features, abnormal pupils, GCS ≤9, rapidly declining consciousness

### Lumbar Puncture Contraindications
- Extensive or rapidly spreading purpura
- Infection at puncture site
- Signs of raised intracranial pressure
- GCS ≤9 or rapidly declining consciousness

### CSF Investigations
- Red and white cell count with differential
- Total protein
- Glucose concentration
- Gram stain microscopy
- Culture and sensitivities
- PCR for relevant pathogens

## Antibiotic Therapy — Bacterial Meningitis
- Before organism identified: ceftriaxone at highest BNF doses
- If contraindicated: cefotaxime
- Add IV amoxicillin if Listeria risk factors present

### Duration by Organism
- Neisseria meningitidis: 5 days
- Haemophilus influenzae type b: 7-10 days
- Streptococcus pneumoniae: 10 days
- Group B streptococcus: 14 days
- Enterobacterales: 21 days
- Listeria monocytogenes: 21 days

## Antibiotic Therapy — Meningococcal Disease
- IV ceftriaxone at highest BNF doses
- Duration: 5 days if recovered

## Corticosteroids
- Bacterial meningitis (>3 months): give IV dexamethasone with or before antibiotics
- Continue if pneumococcus or Hib confirmed; stop for other organisms
- Meningococcal disease: do NOT routinely give corticosteroids

## Key Decision Points for Game Scenarios
- Classic triad (fever + headache + neck stiffness + altered consciousness) may be incomplete
- Non-blanching rash is a red flag but absence does not exclude
- Antibiotics within 1 hour is critical
- Do not delay transfer for pre-hospital antibiotics unless significant delay expected
- LP should not delay antibiotics
- Normal inflammatory markers do NOT rule out meningitis
