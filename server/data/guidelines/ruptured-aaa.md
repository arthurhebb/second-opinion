# Ruptured Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)

## Overview
- Rupture of an abdominal aortic aneurysm (aorta diameter ≥3cm) is a surgical emergency
- Mortality >80% overall; ~50% even in those reaching hospital alive
- Most ruptures are retroperitoneal (contained) initially — allowing a window for intervention

## Recognition

### Classic Triad
- Sudden severe abdominal or back pain
- Hypotension / haemodynamic instability
- Pulsatile abdominal mass

**BUT the complete triad is only present in approximately 50% of cases**

### Presentation Patterns
- Sudden onset central or flank abdominal pain, often radiating to the back
- May present with pain alone initially (contained retroperitoneal rupture)
- Collapse or syncope
- Groin pain (may mimic renal colic)
- Patients may be initially haemodynamically stable (contained leak) then decompensate rapidly

## Commonly Misdiagnosed As
- Renal colic (flank pain, haematuria can occur from ureteric compression)
- Musculoskeletal back pain
- Diverticulitis or other GI emergency
- Perforated viscus
- Mesenteric ischaemia
- MI (especially if hypotensive with ECG changes from shock)

## Investigation
- **CT angiogram** is the diagnostic investigation of choice (if patient is haemodynamically stable enough)
- Bedside ultrasound can confirm AAA presence but cannot reliably confirm or exclude rupture
- Do NOT delay transfer to theatre for imaging in an unstable patient with known AAA and clinical picture of rupture

## Management

### Pre-Operative
- **Permissive hypotension**: target systolic BP 70-90 mmHg while awaiting surgery
- Aggressive fluid resuscitation may worsen bleeding by disrupting clot
- Activate major haemorrhage protocol
- Cross-match at least 6 units packed red cells
- Urgent vascular surgery referral — transfer to vascular centre if needed

### Surgical Repair
- Emergency open surgical repair or endovascular aneurysm repair (EVAR)
- EVAR preferred if anatomically suitable and available (lower peri-operative mortality)
- Open repair if EVAR not suitable or not available

## Screening and Risk Factors
- NHS AAA Screening Programme: one-off ultrasound for men aged 65
- Risk factors: male sex, age >65, smoking (strongest modifiable risk factor), hypertension, family history, peripheral vascular disease, connective tissue disorders

## Common Pitfalls
- Misdiagnosis as renal colic — always consider AAA in men >65 with sudden flank/back pain
- Assuming haemodynamic stability excludes rupture (contained leak can maintain BP initially)
- Over-resuscitating with IV fluids pre-operatively (disrupts clot, worsens bleeding)
- Not palpating the abdomen for pulsatile mass in relevant presentations
- Delay in vascular surgery referral while awaiting investigations
