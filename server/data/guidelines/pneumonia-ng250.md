# NICE NG250 — Pneumonia in Adults

## Diagnosis
- Clinical features: cough, fever, pleuritic chest pain, dyspnoea, sputum production
- Examination: focal crackles, bronchial breathing, dullness to percussion
- Elderly or immunocompromised patients may present atypically (confusion, falls, no fever)

## Investigations

### Chest X-ray
- Chest X-ray within 4 hours of hospital presentation
- Confirms diagnosis and identifies complications (effusion, empyema)
- Consider in community if diagnosis uncertain or no response to treatment

### Blood Tests
- CRP on admission: aids severity assessment and antibiotic decisions
- FBC, U&Es, LFTs
- Blood cultures before antibiotics in moderate-to-severe cases

### Severity Assessment — CURB-65
- **C**onfusion (new onset)
- **U**rea >7 mmol/L
- **R**espiratory rate ≥30/min
- **B**lood pressure: systolic <90 or diastolic ≤60 mmHg
- Age **65** or older

#### Score Interpretation
- **0-1**: Low risk — consider home treatment
- **2**: Intermediate risk — consider hospital admission, short-stay or supervised outpatient
- **3-5**: High risk — admit, consider ICU assessment if score 4-5

## Antibiotic Treatment

### Low Severity (CURB-65 0-1)
- Amoxicillin 500mg TDS for 5 days (oral)
- If penicillin allergic: doxycycline or clarithromycin

### Moderate Severity (CURB-65 2)
- Amoxicillin 500mg TDS ± clarithromycin 500mg BD (oral or IV)
- Dual therapy if atypical pathogen suspected

### High Severity (CURB-65 3-5)
- Co-amoxiclav 1.2g TDS IV + clarithromycin 500mg BD IV
- Consider tazocin if aspiration suspected

### Duration
- Standard course: 5 days
- Extend only if clinically not improving or complications develop
- Review at 48-72 hours: consider step-down from IV to oral (switch criteria: improving, tolerating oral, afebrile for 24hrs)

## Escalation
- Failure to improve at 48-72 hours: review diagnosis, consider imaging, broaden antibiotics
- Parapneumonic effusion or empyema: urgent drainage referral
- ICU referral if: mechanical ventilation needed, septic shock, CURB-65 4-5

## Common Pitfalls
- Attributing pneumonia symptoms to COPD exacerbation without chest X-ray
- Using prolonged antibiotic courses when 5 days is sufficient
- Missing atypical presentation in elderly (confusion without respiratory symptoms)
- Delayed recognition of empyema
